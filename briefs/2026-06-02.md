## Where the puck is going

**Microsoft entering voice as a platform play is the underappreciated risk for pure-plays.** MAI-Voice 2 and MAI-Transcribe 1.5 don't have to be best-in-class to matter — they just need to be good enough for Azure customers who want one vendor. ElevenLabs, Deepgram, and Cartesia all have material Azure-hosted workloads; default integrations in Copilot, Teams, and Azure Speech shift the switching-cost equation. Watch pricing for MAI-Voice 2 specifically: if it undercuts ElevenLabs on per-character, enterprise consolidation accelerates fast.

**The S2S cost argument is collapsing.** The last credible objection to native speech-to-speech was price — OpenAI Realtime at $0.30/min is hard to justify vs. a $0.02/min cascaded pipeline. But Gemini Live at $0.00165/min (100× cheaper than OpenAI Realtime, ~12× cheaper than bundled pipeline APIs) breaks that logic. The practical barrier left is operational: debugging, compliance, and provider lock-in. If Google closes those gaps in H2 2026, the STT→LLM→TTS pipeline becomes a legacy pattern, not a best practice. This is the structural pressure on Deepgram, AssemblyAI, and Cartesia: individual components commoditize as the integrated path gets cheap.

**Latency consistency is the new latency.** Raw TTFA competition is largely settled sub-200ms — the new question is *predictability* at the tail. Gradium's 2ms IQR versus Cartesia Sonic-3's 100ms IQR (same benchmark, same date) is the clearest illustration yet: the production use case for voice agents punishes jitter more than it rewards peak speed. Agents with unpredictable pauses before speaking break conversational trust even if their median is fast. Expect more benchmark providers to add P95/P99 columns and IQR as first-class metrics.
