## TLDR

- **ServiceNow benchmarks frontier ASR on code-switched speech — and finds major gaps** — Published June 9 on HuggingFace, this is the first enterprise-sourced evaluation of ASR on real bilingual customer conversations (Spanish-English, French-English, Canadian French-English, German-English). Seven models tested. ElevenLabs Scribe V2, Gemini 3 Flash, and AssemblyAI Universal-3 Pro surface as top performers. Monolingual architectures suffer 30–50% WER spikes at language-switch boundaries. For anyone building for bilingual markets, the finding is directional: aggregate WER benchmarks hide the worst-case scenarios that actually occur in enterprise customer service. ([HuggingFace / ServiceNow-AI](https://huggingface.co/blog/ServiceNow-AI/code-switching))

## Where the puck is going

**Code-switching is the next STT frontier, and no existing benchmark had measured it until now.** The ServiceNow evaluation is significant not just for the scores, but for the fact that a major enterprise software company had to build the benchmark themselves because one didn't exist. That's the tell: bilingual speech recognition is a hard production problem that hasn't been formally evaluated by any of the incumbent STT providers or independent benchmarking services. The finding — that frontier models degrade 30–50% at language boundaries — explains a real enterprise pain point that aggregate-WER benchmarks invisibilize. ElevenLabs Scribe V2 and AssemblyAI Universal-3 Pro topping the code-switching leaderboard is a surprise given that both are positioned primarily as quality/accuracy leaders rather than multilingual specialists. This may be because their underlying architectures handle subword-level language mixing better than end-to-end models optimized for clean monolingual inputs. For builders targeting bilingual contact centers, Latin American markets, or any deployment where code-switching is common, this benchmark should be the procurement input — not the standard WER leaderboards.

**ElevenLabs winning the Anthropic Cookbook recommendation is a distribution signal, not just a brand win.** The speech engine BYOL strategy — "we handle voice, you handle the LLM" — only works if it gets embedded in the default developer patterns that engineers reach for when building voice into Claude. Anthropic's Cookbook recipes are exactly those defaults: developers copy them, startups start on them, and enterprise builders model procurement on them. The fact that Scribe v2 (not Deepgram, not Whisper, not AssemblyAI) got the STT slot in the official Anthropic reference architecture is a compounding distribution advantage: every Claude-based voice app started by a developer who followed the Cookbook starts with ElevenLabs. This validates the prediction from the June 9 brief that the session-stack race would be decided not by raw benchmark scores but by which stack becomes the default plumbing around the LLMs that win.
