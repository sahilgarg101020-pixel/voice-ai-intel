## Where the puck is going

**Instruction following in long, multi-turn conversations is the next production quality axis — and it doesn't have a real benchmark yet.** The Pipecat/Ultravox conversation from Coval surfaces a gap that every builder building real workflows has hit: frontier models score well on standard benchmarks but production stacks are running 18-month-old models because the evaluation cost to validate a switch is too high without trust-worthy voice-specific evals. What's missing isn't raw benchmark scores — it's evaluation of the things that make voice feel broken in practice: does the agent remember a constraint from turn 3 when answering a tool call at turn 15? Does it maintain persona under disfluency and interruption? Does it back-channel appropriately? Kwindla's aiewf-eval is the earliest public attempt to build this, but it's still open source and early. The builder who ships a production-validated, voice-specific instruction-following eval framework — not a latency benchmark, not WER — owns the procurement conversation for the next 12 months. This connects directly to the clinic operator signal from June 12 (22-point CSAT jump from interruption reduction, not WER) and the general trend of evaluation maturity lagging deployment reality.

**Diarization has become a competitive axis almost overnight.** Two weeks ago, speaker attribution was treated as a solved commodity by most voice stacks. Then Deepgram shipped Batch Diarization V2 with 3.3x human preference and ~80% CER reduction (June 12), and now the surfaced AssemblyAI streaming upgrade posts 51% fewer false-alarm speakers and 80% fewer phantom turns vs Deepgram Nova-3. These aren't incremental improvements — they're catching and eliminating entire classes of production failure. The distinction matters for builders: Deepgram's upgrade is batch (post-call analytics, compliance, coaching), AssemblyAI's is streaming (real-time speaker identification in live voice agents). Different use cases, both now genuinely better. For anyone building multi-party voice agents — sales calls, meeting assistants, multi-party support — diarization quality needs to re-enter the vendor evaluation matrix.

## Signals from the field

- **Kwindla Hultman Kramer (Pipecat, via Coval):** *"Most production systems still run 18-month-old models because switching is expensive and evaluation is hard."* — From the infrastructure layer, this is the clearest signal that benchmark proliferation hasn't actually solved the procurement problem for voice. The limiting factor is not model quality; it's the cost of validating a model switch without a trusted evaluation framework. ([Coval blog](https://www.coval.dev/blog/the-state-of-voice-ai-instruction-following-in-2026-a-conversation-with-kwindla-from-pipecat-and-zach-from-ultravox))
- **Zach Koch (Ultravox, via Coval):** *"Speech-to-speech models solve the latency problem but fail at complex instruction following. Cascaded pipelines solve instruction following but add latency on every hop. The unsolved problem is having both."* — Ultravox's positioning is that its model bridges this gap (speech-native but instruction-following capable), but the framing of the underlying problem is accurate regardless of who solves it.
- **AssemblyAI (blog, June 2):** *"We're not just improving a number — we're removing an entire class of failure from live voice agent sessions."* — Phantom speaker hallucination (attributing words to a speaker who wasn't present) is one of those bugs that doesn't show up in demos and wrecks trust in production. The 80% reduction in phantom turns is the number that matters for contact-center deployments.
