## Where the puck is going

**ElevenLabs is assembling a complete voice infrastructure stack — and the Hermes demo made it visible.** In three weeks: Speech Engine (BYOL voice session wrapper, June 5), Scribe v2 Realtime as the native Agents STT (this week), and the Hermes Agent demo confirming telephony is in production (June 4, 927K impressions). The play is explicit: own every layer of the voice session so developers plug in one SDK and ElevenLabs becomes the mandatory voice plumbing around any LLM. Each layer — Scribe for STT, Speech Engine for turn-taking and LLM proxying, Flash v2.5 for TTS, ElevenAgents for SIP/Twilio telephony — adds switching cost on its own. Combined, they become a session moat. Competitors selling individual TTS or STT APIs are increasingly bidding on commodity line items while ElevenLabs prices the bundle. The 3,157 bookmarks on the Hermes demo are the tell: that's intent-to-build, not passive engagement.

**The phone call is the primary voice agent surface — and the underlying economics are harder than any benchmark shows.** The Hermes engagement confirms that developers and end-users respond to voice agents that accept real calls, not web-embedded widgets. But the SIP signal from this morning surfaces a blind spot the whole industry shares: at 1,000 concurrent calls, $0.008/min wholesale dial cost in Southeast Asia runs to ~$2,880/day in carrier spend before a single token or TTS byte is priced. No voice AI benchmark or pricing calculator published by any vendor includes this layer. Builders going into phone-native deployments — outbound sales dialers, inbound support, IVR replacement — need to model total COGS: SIP trunk + STT + TTS + LLM + orchestration. The gap between "API pricing" and "what this actually costs to run" widens fast at scale, and it's where voice agent margins get compressed.
