## Where the puck is going

**Speaker attribution is the overlooked production metric.** Deepgram's Batch Diarization V2 — 3.3x human preference, ~80% median CER reduction on contact-center audio — lands after a period where aggregate WER has dominated benchmark coverage. But for any call-center, multi-party compliance, or AI coaching use case, getting *who said what* wrong is as expensive as getting *what was said* wrong. The V2 release shifts diarization from "good enough legacy feature" to an active quality battleground. With word-level speaker labels (not just turn-level), it's the first time production diarization is granular enough to catch mid-turn interjections accurately. Expect other STT vendors to respond with their own diarization benchmarks within weeks; the category just got a clear bar to clear.

**Enterprise voice is splitting into a custom-engineering track and a platform track — and the custom track is where the big contracts live.** The Deepgram restaurant deployments are the clearest illustration this cycle: these aren't API customers using a developer portal. They're buying fine-tuned models, noise filtering tuned to their specific acoustic environments, live menu integrations, custom routing, and a forward-deployed engineer. That's closer to a professional-services engagement than a subscription. This pattern — vertical-specialized voice AI requiring significant integration depth — was implied by the AethexAI Africa/MENA thesis (June 4) and now confirmed in a major US consumer vertical. The strategic question for builders: the platform track (Vapi/Retell/ElevenAgents) wins developer volume; the custom-engineering track wins enterprise ACV. They may not be the same product.

**Voice AI has a two-sided security problem that no vendor has addressed publicly.** On one side: AI voice agents being hijacked by inaudible adversarial audio embedded in ambient content (AudioHijack, day 8 — still no vendor response). On the other: human employees being deceived by AI-generated executive voice clones used to authorize wire transfers and system access (Teams vishing, active in June 2026). Both attack surfaces trace back to the same underlying capability: voice AI models that produce indistinguishable speech from minimal input. The enterprise security posture question is no longer "can this TTS model be cloned?" — it's "what operational controls prevent both vectors simultaneously?" No vendor has published an answer. The first one to do so owns the regulated-enterprise narrative for the next 12 months.
