## Where the puck is going

**Sovereign voice AI is now a three-continent trend.** In the span of two weeks: Poland took an $11M equity stake in ElevenLabs (June 17), India's government-backed IndiaAI Mission produced Gnani Prisma v2.5 (June 19), and Deepgram's Australia endpoint reached GA (June 17). Each signal is different — government equity investment, national mission-funded model development, and infrastructure data residency — but they point to the same conclusion: voice AI is being treated as sovereign infrastructure, not just a developer API. For builders, the implication is that compliance and data residency are hardening from "nice to have" into procurement blockers. The winning position isn't just lowest latency or best WER — it's lowest latency *within jurisdictional boundaries*.

The Gnani story is particularly instructive. Their claim of 15-18% WER improvement over ElevenLabs, Deepgram, and Sarvam on noisy Indian speech validates the thesis that regional training data creates a genuine accuracy moat for local conditions. But recall the June 17 signal: India's PhysicsWallah reportedly chose ElevenLabs over locally-funded Sarvam AI ($234M) for production deployment. The takeaway for founders: sovereign models win local benchmarks, but enterprise procurement still prioritizes global quality, latency, and reliability over regional specialization — until regulatory requirements force the switch. The trigger isn't quality parity; it's compliance mandates.

**STT is fragmenting into regional specialists faster than the market tracks.** Gladia owns European business audio (June 18), Gnani owns Indian dialects (June 19), Soniox owns multilingual telephony (June 8), and NVIDIA Nemotron owns self-hosted deployments (June 11). Aggregate WER leaderboards — still the default procurement tool — are actively misleading. A vendor scoring 6% average WER can score 18% on your specific audio type. The builder opportunity: vertical-specific STT evaluation tooling that benchmarks on *your* audio, not theirs.
